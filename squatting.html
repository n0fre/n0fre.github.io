Jean-Jacques Rousseau and Squatting Rights

Introduction

Squatting refers to the occupation of unused or derelict buildings or land without the permission of the owner. Through a process known as adverse possession, squatters attempt to gain legal title to a piece of property. {!Pruijt outlines five basic reasons why people squat: (i) out of deprivation...}[Pruijt, Hans. “Squatting in Europe.” Social Movement Studies 12.1 (2013): 1–17. doi: 10.1080/14742837.2012.732674.] {!United Nations estimates are that 800 to 900 million people worldwide are technically squatters}[UN-Habitat. “Streets as Tools for Urban Transformation in Slums.” 2013.] Despite this, the subject is rarely discussed in political or academic debates.

In the contractualist approach as outlined in his The Social Contract, {!Rousseau discusses his social contract theory as a social pact...}[Rousseau, Jean-Jacques. The Social Contract. Trans. Christopher Betts. Oxford University Press, 1994.] I do not interpret the general will here in its direct democratic interpretation... but an embodiment of the citizens' common interests that are approximated through legislations in a representative democracy.

In this essay, I will defend the view that squatting is justified through the social contract theory as outlined by Jean-Jacques Rousseau. First, I will argue that squatters, rather than owners of unused property, have a right of first occupancy. Second, I will argue that any legal title pertaining to unused property is illegitimate due to it not being in favour of the general will. Third, I will argue that squatting is justified because it is a form of direct action against a violation of the social contract. Lastly, I will anticipate certain objections to my thesis...

Main body

Argument 1: squatters have the right of first occupancy

In book 1, chapter 9 of The Social Contract, {!Rousseau sets out to answer how a claim on private property can be justified...}[Rousseau, Jean-Jacques. The Social Contract. 1994.] According to Rousseau, a claim on private property can be justified by legal title and by the right of first occupancy, the latter of which must meet three conditions:

  • The land must not be inhabited by someone else.
  • No more must be occupied than is needed for subsistence.
  • Possession must be taken not by empty ceremonies, but by work and cultivation.

Rousseau does not explain in this chapter what he explicitly means by legal title, but he does emphasize his argument... ‘the right that each individual has over his property is always subordinate to the right that the community has over everyone’ (p.62).

The doctrine of adverse possession exists to encourage and reward productive uses of space by countering needless vacancy... {!Examples: No Borders Kitchen Lesvos, Nottingham Squatting Project, Amsterdam cultural squats}[No Borders Kitchen, 2017; Lodge, 2005; Dee, E.T.C., 2015.]
